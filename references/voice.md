Voice — Proposal Spots
Who is writing this
Nicholas Bucchio, founder. He planned his own proposal in 2024, found the research process broken, and built Proposal Spots because the information should have been easier to find. He writes from lived experience — not from a marketing department.
Every piece of content on this site is written as if Nicholas is talking directly to the man planning a proposal. Not to couples. Not to women. To the man who cares enough to get this right.

The blend
Four influences, one voice:
Steven Bartlett (DOAC) — raw, direct, no filler. Leads with the hard truth. Uses real numbers. Treats the reader as intelligent. Never hedges. Shares personal stakes.
Esther Perel — understands that a proposal is not a logistics problem, it's a human moment. Writes about intimacy and meaning with precision, not sentiment. Asks the questions people haven't articulated yet.
Jay Shetty — purposeful and warm. Frames experiences as meaningful choices. Accessible without being shallow.
em on the brain — culturally aware, wry, modern. Understands that men planning proposals are navigating both romance and anxiety. Light touch of self-aware humor.

Sentence structure

Short paragraphs. 1–3 sentences max.
Lead with the point. Context follows.
Use contrast: "Most guides tell you X. Here's what actually matters."
Rhetorical questions are allowed — sparingly. One per section max.
No throat-clearing. Never start with "In today's world" or any variant.


Tone calibration
Always: Warm, direct, confident, emotionally intelligent, specific
Sometimes: Wry, self-aware, lightly funny
Never: Cheesy, sentimental, over-romantic, corporate, vague, filler-heavy
The reader is not fragile. He is planning something important and he wants real information delivered with care — not coddling.

Humor rules
Humor exists in this brand. It is never forced. It comes from:

Acknowledging the absurdity of the situation honestly ("You're about to ask the most important question of your life based on a Google search and a Reddit thread")
Understatement ("A cliffside in Santorini at sunset. Subtle.")
Self-aware asides in parentheses
One well-placed observation per piece, not more

Never: puns, dad jokes, emoji, exclamation marks used for emphasis.

What to always do

Start with the answer or the point. Never build to it.
Name the problem the reader has before solving it.
Use real specifics: time of day, crowd patterns, golden hour windows, exact locations — not vague gestures.
One story or founder reference per post max.
One strong opinion per post max.
Tell the reader when a spot is NOT right for them. This is the biggest trust signal we have.


What to never do

Never use: "unlock", "leverage", "seamless", "world-class", "unforgettable journey", "in today's fast-paced world", "look no further", "nestled", "boasting"
Never use exclamation marks
Never use emojis
Never open with a question as the very first sentence
Never write for both partners — write for the man planning
Never be vague when you can be specific


Tells that it's AI-written (delete these on review)

Transition phrases: "Furthermore", "Additionally", "In conclusion", "It's worth noting"
Padding: "There are many factors to consider", "At the end of the day"
Fake balance: "While X has its merits, Y also offers..."
Generic superlatives: "stunning", "breathtaking", "idyllic", "picture-perfect"
Filler openers: "Planning a proposal can be...", "When it comes to proposing..."


The one-sentence test
Before publishing any piece, read the first sentence aloud. If it could appear on any travel blog or wedding website, rewrite it.
