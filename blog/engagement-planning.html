Engagement planning: what to do after the yes

Nicholas Bucchio, Founder·May 20, 2026·7 min read

The proposal is the moment. Engagement planning is the six months that follow. Most of what gets written about it focuses on the wedding. The actually useful first month has very little to do with venues or save-the-dates. It is about handling the announcement well, getting the ring resized, calling the right people in the right order, and not letting Instagram set the pace.

This is a calm timeline of what to do after the yes, organised by the first 48 hours, the first month, and the first six months.

The first 48 hours

The most common mistake in this window is rushing the public part. Slow down.

Take the first hour for the two of you. Before any phone call. Look at the ring together in real light. Eat something. Sit somewhere. The first hour of being engaged is one you will remember more clearly than the next month combined. Do not spend it on your phones.

Call family in the right order. Her parents first, your parents second, the closest siblings and grandparents third. Voice call, not text. The conversations are short, often emotional, and worth far more than a group message would be.

Tell three or four closest friends within 24 hours. The people who should not learn about the engagement from a social media post. Call or message individually. Avoid group chats for this; they remove the personal note.

Hold off on social media until day two or three. Most regret about how an engagement gets announced is about rushing the public part. Wait until you have a few photos you actually like, the calls are done, and you both feel ready. There is no penalty for waiting.

Save the proposal photos to a backup immediately. Whatever the photographer sent. Whatever you took on your phone. Backed up to a cloud service, an external drive, or both. The photos exist in one place when you receive them and that place is fragile.

The first week

Slightly slower now. The adrenaline is wearing off.

Resize the ring. Most rings need a half-size adjustment within the first month. Take it back to the original jeweller. Resize takes one to three weeks at most reputable shops. She wears the ring in the meantime if it fits well enough, takes it off only when she'd want to anyway.

Insure the ring properly. Standard home insurance rarely covers jewellery beyond a low cap, often $1,500 to $2,500. A personal articles policy or a jewellery rider adds proper coverage. Expect $1 to $2 of premium per $100 of insured value annually. Get the policy in place within the first two weeks. The ring is uninsured between the proposal and that paperwork.

Pick one photo you both love and post it. Or do not post. Either is fine. If you do post, less caption is better. The photo carries the announcement.

Talk briefly about the wedding date window. Not the date. The season. Spring, late summer, autumn. Next year or the year after. This conversation is short and useful. It is also the one your families will start asking about within the week.

The first month

The transition from engagement-as-moment to engagement-as-project.

Agree on the three numbers that decide everything else. Date window, guest count band, total budget. Have this conversation before talking to a single vendor or visiting a single venue. Every later decision flows from these three. Most early-engagement stress comes from skipping this conversation and trying to evaluate venues without it.

Decide whether you want a planner. A wedding planner is genuinely useful when the guest count is over 80, the venue is unusual or destination, or one of you has a job that does not give you time to project-manage a wedding. Skip if the wedding is small, local, and the two of you have project-managed things at work before. Our proposal planner guide covers the buy vs. build decision in detail; the same logic applies to wedding planning.

Have one in-person dinner with both families together if they have not met. Low-stakes, no agenda beyond meeting. Do this in the first month before the wedding-planning energy raises the temperature.

Block out the next three months on the calendar. If you both have jobs, the next quarter is when you make most of the big decisions. Block the time before it gets eaten by everything else.

The first three months

Real decisions start landing.

Lock the venue and the date. The venue locks the date. Most venues book six to fourteen months out. Sooner if you want a popular Saturday in peak season. Be willing to take a Friday or Sunday to cut the price meaningfully.

Book the photographer second. Good wedding photographers book out almost as far as venues. The photographer you actually want is harder to secure than the second-tier ones. Book them before considering catering, flowers, or music. For the photographer brief logic that applies to weddings too, see our destination engagement photographer guide.

Have the money conversation properly. Total budget, who pays for what, what each family has offered. The conversation is uncomfortable for about ten minutes and then becomes a relief. Avoiding it for three months is the more painful path.

Consider an engagement party, or do not. Engagement parties are common but optional. Skip if the same guest list would be invited twice (once now, once at the wedding). Throw one if your families have not met, you want a low-stakes evening with the people who matter, or it is a tradition you both genuinely care about.

The first six months

By this point the wedding is real on a calendar.

Send save-the-dates. Six to eight months before the wedding. Earlier if you have many international guests. The save-the-date is information, not invitation. Keep it simple: date, city, formal invitation to follow.

Decide on the wedding party. Bridesmaids, groomsmen, family roles. The conversation about who is and is not in the wedding party has consequences. Do not extend it just to be nice.

Do the engagement shoot, if you want one. Often a one-hour session with the wedding photographer, four to eight months before the wedding. Useful for save-the-dates, the wedding website, and getting comfortable being photographed together before the day itself.

Take a long weekend together, alone. Three or four months into the engagement. Go somewhere quiet. Talk about anything other than the wedding. The engagement is supposed to be a phase of life, not a project plan with a deliverable. The couples who treat it like a project tend to arrive at the wedding exhausted by it.

What to skip in the first six months

A short list of common engagement-planning moves that mostly drain energy.

  • Hiring a planner before you have agreed on the three numbers. Useless. The planner will be running ahead of decisions you have not made.
  • Touring more than five venues. Diminishing returns set in fast. Most couples pick from the second or third venue.
  • Building a Pinterest board with 200 pins. Aesthetic overwhelm. Cap it at thirty.
  • Inviting friends to weigh in on the dress, the suit, or the rings. Their opinions are not free of charge; they cost you weeks of indecision.
  • Booking a destination wedding before you have asked the closest twenty people if they can come. Half can usually not. The destination is fine. The conversation has to happen first.
  • Public engagement countdowns on social media. Performance, not relationship.

The short version

The first 48 hours: take the moment slowly, call family, hold off on social.

The first week: resize and insure the ring, post when ready, agree on a season.

The first month: lock the three numbers, decide on a planner, meet the families.

The first three months: book venue, book photographer, have the money conversation.

The first six months: save-the-dates, wedding party, engagement shoot, a quiet weekend with no wedding talk.

The engagement is supposed to feel like a phase of life. Plan it slowly enough that it actually does.

Frequently asked

What should you do first after getting engaged?

Take a slow hour with your fiancée before doing anything else. Look at the ring together, talk, breathe, eat something. Then call the small handful of family members who should hear it from you directly, in this order: her parents, your parents, the closest siblings. Hold off on social media for at least 24 to 48 hours. Most regret about how an engagement gets announced comes from rushing the public part.

How long should you be engaged before getting married?

The honest answer is 12 to 18 months for most couples. Long enough to plan a real wedding without rushing logistics, short enough to keep momentum. Engagements under six months work if you want a small wedding and have flexibility. Engagements over two years often lose energy unless there is a specific reason for the wait.

What is the first thing to do when planning an engagement?

Decide together on three things before any vendor conversation: the wedding date window (season, not exact date), the rough guest count band (50, 100, 200), and the total budget you are both comfortable with. Every later decision flows from those three. Most early-engagement stress comes from skipping this conversation.

When should you announce your engagement publicly?

Most couples post on social media within the first week, often within 48 hours. There is no rule. Tell family first. Tell close friends second. Post when you both feel ready. The right time is when you have a few photos you actually like and have called the people who should not learn the news from a feed.

What documents or admin do you need to handle after getting engaged?

Resize the ring with the original jeweller (most rings need adjustment within the first month). Insure the ring on a personal articles policy or jewellery rider; jewellery is rarely fully covered under standard home insurance. Update your emergency contacts. Save the proposal photos to a backup the moment you have them. Everything else, including legal name change and beneficiary updates, can wait until closer to the wedding.

Do you need an engagement party?

No. Engagement parties are common, not required. Skip if you have a small wedding planned and the same guest list would be invited twice. Throw one if your families have not met, if you want a low-stakes evening with the people who matter, or if it is a tradition you both genuinely care about. The proposal itself was the celebration that mattered. Anything else is optional.
