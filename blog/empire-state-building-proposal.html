The Empire State Building Proposal: What Happened and What to Take From It

Nicholas Bucchio, Founder·July 1, 2026·7 min read

On the afternoon of July 1, 2026, two masked climbers reached the top of the Empire State Building's transmission tower, unfurled a banner, and one of them proposed to the other on a lower platform before both were taken into custody by the NYPD. Reporting from CBS News New York and the BBC confirms both the climb and the proposal itself. This is what happened, what the story teaches you about planning a proposal, and how to actually propose at the Empire State Building without ending the day in handcuffs.

Two people scaled the Empire State Building's 1,454-foot spire around noon on July 1, 2026, unfurled a peace banner near the top, and one proposed to the other on a lower platform during the descent. Both were taken into custody around 1pm without injury. Potential charges include trespass and reckless endangerment. The Empire State Building already sells an official proposal package at its observation decks, which is the version of this moment that does not end at a precinct. The lesson for anyone planning a big proposal is not that you need to go bigger. It is that the format has to match the two people in the moment.

What happened

According to reporting from CBS News New York and the BBC, the climb began around noon on Wednesday, July 1, 2026. Two people, reported as a man and a woman, were seen on the observation deck before scaling the transmission tower above it. Witnesses initially assumed they had authorization. The pair unfurled a large banner near the top of the tower with a message paraphrasing a widely quoted line often attributed to Jimi Hendrix: "When the power of love beats the love of power the world knows peace."

Around 12:35pm they began descending. At one of the lower platforms of the spire, the man dropped to one knee and appeared to propose. The two embraced, the woman removed her mask, and they kissed. Both were taken into custody around 1pm. No one was injured. Police sources cited potential charges of trespass and reckless endangerment.

The winds at the top were reported at 5 to 10 mph. Temperature at the observation deck was around 89 degrees Fahrenheit. The transmission tower is not a climbing structure. It carries live radio and television transmission for local stations. A former CBS engineer quoted by CBS New York described the environment as high-RF, high-voltage, and dangerous for anyone not trained specifically for tower work.

The building's response

The Empire State Building's spokesperson released a statement calling the incident an unauthorized event, confirming no danger to tenants, visitors, or observation deck guests, and then, in the same statement, pivoted to marketing. The building emphasized that its own observation deck offers a practical way to plan a memorable marriage proposal.

That pivot is worth pausing on. A building that had just been climbed without authorization by two people who were then arrested used the same press statement to promote its official proposal package. The commercial instinct is not wrong. The observation deck genuinely is a well-run proposal venue and has been for years. It is also a reminder that every viral proposal moment is also, immediately, a marketing opportunity for someone. Sometimes that someone is the building.

The take: big public spectacle is not the lesson here

Most proposal advice from the last week of this story will be about scale. Go bigger. Match the moment. Chase the viral photograph. That is the wrong lesson.

The right lesson is that this proposal worked for two specific people who share a specific relationship to public statement, to risk, and probably to each other's tolerance for both. It is going to be part of their story for the rest of their lives, and if the two of them are actually the kind of couple who thrives on shared public risk, it may become one of the great engagement stories of the year. For any other couple who tries to replicate it, or a lighter version of it, the story ends differently: a partner who was surprised in the wrong direction, a moment they wish they could have back, or a photo that never quite lives up to the intention.

Privacy is underrated in proposal planning, and it is underrated precisely because these are the stories that go viral. The quiet proposals do not trend. They do not need to. They land right for the two people who are in them and are remembered as inevitable rather than staged.

A great proposal fits the two people who are in it. A viral proposal fits the algorithm. They are almost never the same moment.

Who a big public proposal actually works for

There are couples for whom this format is the right answer. Almost all of them share a few things in common.

  • Both people have a genuine relationship to public performance or public statement (activists, athletes, performers, content creators who both post as a couple already)
  • The couple has already navigated at least one meaningful public moment together and both remember it fondly, not with regret
  • Neither partner is prone to intense discomfort with being watched by strangers
  • The gesture is legitimately connected to who they are, not borrowed from a viral clip
  • Both are aligned on the risk (physical, legal, professional) that comes with it

If your relationship checks all five, a big public proposal may genuinely be the format. For everyone else, it is not.

Who it does not work for

The much larger group. If your partner has ever mentioned that they hate being the center of attention at a restaurant, if either of you would find flash mobs stressful, if the two of you enjoy your relationship best when it is private and observed by very few people, a public spectacle proposal is not the right instrument. This is true regardless of how much the couple in the news made it look like something to copy.

If you are stuck between wanting a great story to tell later and wanting the moment itself to be right, the moment wins. The story becomes what the moment was, not what the format looked like from a helicopter camera.

How to actually propose at the Empire State Building

You can do it. It just does not require climbing anything or being taken into custody. The Empire State Building operates official proposal packages at its 86th floor and 102nd floor observation decks. The packages include reserved timing, dedicated staff, discreet photography arrangements, and access to specific vantage points at hours when the deck is less crowded. Pricing scales from a few hundred dollars for a basic reservation to several thousand for full private-access packages at premium hours.

The best time is at blue hour, roughly 20 to 40 minutes after sunset in summer, when the city goes from gold to blue and the interior lights start to dominate the frame. Weekday evenings are meaningfully quieter than weekend evenings. Book through the Empire State Building's own website rather than a third-party reseller. The building has an in-house team who runs these bookings and they are much better than the equivalent hotel package elsewhere in the city.

The founder's note

When I planned my own proposal in 2024, I spent one long evening thinking through whether the version of the day I wanted was actually the version she wanted. It was not the same version. The one she would have loved was quieter and smaller than what I had been imagining, and quieter than the version I thought made for the better story. She was right. The moment we ended up with was the right one because it fit her, not because it fit a headline. The Empire State Building climb this week is a genuinely dramatic story and it may be the perfect story for the couple in it. It is not the template. The template is that the format has to fit the person.

Frequently asked

Who climbed the Empire State Building on July 1, 2026?

Two masked climbers, reported to be a man and a woman, scaled the Empire State Building's transmission tower shortly after noon on July 1, 2026. Their identities were not immediately released by the NYPD. They were taken into custody around 1pm after descending. Potential charges being considered include trespass and reckless endangerment. No one was injured.

Did the climbers actually get engaged?

Based on the footage and eyewitness reporting from CBS News New York and the BBC, yes. After unfurling a banner at the top, one climber descended to a lower platform on the spire, dropped to one knee, and presented a ring. They embraced, the woman removed her mask, and they kissed. Then they continued their descent and were taken into custody.

What did the banner say?

The banner read: When the power of love beats the love of power the world knows peace. It is a variation on a widely quoted line often attributed to Jimi Hendrix. The climbers unfurled it near the top of the spire before descending to the platform where the proposal happened.

Can you legally propose at the Empire State Building?

Yes, and the Empire State Building actively markets its 86th floor and 102nd floor observation decks for proposals, including a dedicated proposal package. The package includes reserved timing, discreet staff support, and access at less crowded hours. Book through the Empire State Building's official website rather than a reseller. How tall is the Empire State Building and where did the proposal happen?

The top occupied floor sits at 1,250 feet. Including the transmission tower on top, the building reaches 1,454 feet. The banner was unfurled near the top of the spire, and the proposal itself happened on a lower platform of the tower on the descent, well above the observation decks.

Is a big public spectacle proposal a good idea?

For a small number of couples where both people genuinely thrive on shared public statement, yes. For everyone else, no. Big public proposals shift the moment from a private exchange between two people to a performance for strangers, and the majority of the couples we hear from later say they wish it had been quieter. Match the format to the person, not to the story you want to tell later.
