How to Buy Wedding Proposal Packages: A Buyer's Guide

Nicholas Bucchio, Founder·June 9, 2026·9 min read

Wedding proposal packages cost between $600 and $10,000, depending on whether you are buying a styled setup or full-service planning with a venue. To buy one well, you need to know what is in the box before you pay a deposit, because the gap between a $600 package and a $6,000 package is mostly invisible from a product page. This guide walks through the four tiers worth knowing, the buying process from inquiry to day-of, the red flags that mean walk away, and the questions to ask before you wire any money.

Most proposal packages fall into four tiers: styled setup ($300 to $900), full design and decor ($1,500 to $3,500), full-service planning with venue ($3,500 to $8,000), and destination or luxury ($8,000 and up). The price difference is rarely about what is on the table. It is about who books the venue, who manages the vendors, and who takes responsibility when something goes wrong. The single best filter when comparing two packages at similar price points is whether the planner will name the specific venue before deposit, or whether they expect you to bring one.

What you are actually buying

A proposal package is a bundle. The components vary, but most include some combination of decor and styling, a venue or coordination with one, photography or videography, a planner who handles vendor logistics, and day-of execution.

The thing to understand is that "package" is a marketing word, not a defined product. One company's $2,000 package and another's $2,000 package can be wildly different. The cheap one might be decor only, with you sourcing the location, the photographer, and the timing yourself. The more expensive one might include the planner doing all of that for you. The price often looks identical. The labor included does not.

If you want the full breakdown of what each tier of package typically contains and why, our deeper post on proposal packages covers the value side of the equation. This guide focuses on the buying side: how to evaluate one, how to negotiate it, and how to know when to walk.

The four tiers worth knowing

Tier 1: Styled setup ($300 to $900)

You get a designed setup (florals, candles, signage, sometimes seating or a rug) delivered to a location you have already chosen, set up before you arrive, and removed afterward. No venue. No photographer. No day-of coordination beyond the setup itself. This is the best value in the entire category if your location is already handled. You are paying for the styling, not the planning.

Tier 2: Full design and decor ($1,500 to $3,500)

Tier 1 plus a photographer, sometimes a videographer, and a coordinator who manages the timing between the setup and your arrival. This is the most common package sold, and the added cost over tier 1 is mostly the photographer. If you book a photographer directly and trust yourself with the timing, you can often replicate this tier for less. You also take on the risk.

Tier 3: Full-service planning with venue ($3,500 to $8,000)

The planner sources and books the venue, handles permits if needed, manages every vendor, and runs the day. You show up. This is where the price starts to make real sense, because a planner with venue relationships can get you into spaces you cannot book directly. That alone is sometimes worth the difference.

Tier 4: Destination and luxury ($8,000 and up)

Tier 3 plus travel coordination, accommodations, and often a multi-day surrounding experience. Yachts, private estates, chef-led dinners, musicians, helicopter access. A small minority of proposals justify this. If you can name the specific reasons your proposal needs a production at this level, this is for you. If you cannot, you are buying status, not a better moment.

The buying process, step by step

Step 1: Inquiry. Most planners have a contact form that triggers a 20 to 30 minute discovery call. The good ones use this call to understand the relationship and the partner. The mediocre ones use it to upsell.

Step 2: Quote. Within a few business days you receive a written quote. Read every line. If something is "to be confirmed" or "to be discussed", get it confirmed in writing before you pay anything.

Step 3: Deposit. Industry standard is 30 to 50 percent non-refundable to hold the date. Reputable planners refund the deposit if they cancel. They do not refund it if you cancel. Both should be in the contract in plain English.

Step 4: Custom questionnaire. The good firms ask 20 to 40 questions about her, your relationship, and the moment you want to create. The bad ones send you a Pinterest board and ask you to pick a "style".

Step 5: Planning phase. Two to eight weeks of vendor coordination, location confirmations, and design sign-off. You should hear from the planner at least once a week with concrete updates, not vague reassurance.

Step 6: Day of. You arrive. They handle everything else. If on the day itself you have to handle anything beyond showing up with the ring, the package was oversold.

Red flags that mean walk away

  • No price posted anywhere on the site, and the discovery call dodges the question
  • Heavy use of the word "bespoke" with no specifics under it
  • The same setup photo appears across multiple "different" packages
  • No clear cancellation policy or contract template available before deposit
  • A request for full payment upfront
  • Refusal to name the specific venue before deposit (for tier 3 and tier 4)
  • Reviews that all sound alike and are dated within a few weeks of each other
  • The lead planner on the website is not the person you would actually work with

Questions to ask before paying a deposit

  1. What exactly is included, line by line, with no "TBD" entries?
  2. Who is my day-of contact, and is that person the one I am speaking to now?
  3. How many proposals have you executed in this exact venue or location?
  4. Can I see photos from the last three proposals you did (not the highlight reel)?
  5. What happens if weather forces a change? Is there a backup, and is the cost included?
  6. What is the refund policy if I cancel? If you cancel?
  7. Are vendor fees included or billed separately, and what is the realistic ceiling on extras?
  8. If something goes wrong on the day, who fixes it, and at whose cost?

The last question is the one most buyers skip. It is also the one that separates a $1,500 package from an $8,000 package more than anything else on the invoice.

"Bespoke" with no specifics is a price tag with no product.

When you do not need a package at all

Most proposal packages at hotels are theater. A bottle of champagne on the bed, rose petals on the floor, and a card that says "Congratulations" before the question has been asked. The hotel charges $400 for what is essentially room service with garnish, and the version of the category that has earned the eye-roll. This is opinion #3 of the brand, and we have not yet been talked out of it.

A good package does the opposite. It removes the parts of the day you would have handled badly: vendor coordination, timing, decor, weather contingency. You can plan an excellent proposal without any of that. You can also pay for a package that includes all of it and have the best version of the day. What you should not do is pay package-level money for hotel-level execution.

If your proposal is happening at home, in a familiar place, with a photographer you already know and a budget under $1,000, you probably do not need a package. You need an evening that is yours and a plan you can hold in your head. Our guide to how to plan a proposal set-up covers this version of the day. If your proposal is happening in a city you do not live in, with vendors you have never met, on a date that cannot move, you almost certainly do.

The founder's note

When I started planning my own proposal in 2024, I priced packages from six different planners. The quotes ranged from $1,400 to $11,000 for what looked, on paper, like similar bundles. The difference was almost entirely in what each planner was prepared to take responsibility for. The cheap end stopped at "we will deliver and set up". The expensive end included "we will solve any problem that comes up between deposit and the moment she says yes". That difference is the entire pricing logic of this category. Buy the level of responsibility you actually need, not the level the website thinks you should want.

If you are buying for a destination, the questions get harder. Our guide to the best destinations to propose covers where each kind of trip earns the spend, and the destination pages themselves (for example Italy or Hawaii) get specific on where the moment can actually happen.

Frequently asked

How much should a wedding proposal package cost?

Between $600 and $10,000 for the vast majority of buyers. A styled setup is usually $300 to $900. A full-service package with venue and photography is $3,500 to $8,000. Above $10,000 is almost always destination work or a custom production with named vendors. If you are quoted more than $10,000 for a domestic proposal at a venue you could book yourself, ask for the line-item breakdown before doing anything else.

Are hotel proposal packages worth it?

Usually not. Most hotel proposal packages are a bottle of champagne, a small floral arrangement, and a printed card for $300 to $500. They exist to drive a room booking, not to design a moment. The exceptions are hotels with a genuine in-house planner and a track record. Ask to speak to that planner directly before you book the package, not after.

What is the difference between a proposal planner and a proposal package?

A proposal package is a fixed bundle of services at a set price. A proposal planner is a person you hire by the hour or by the project to design something custom. Packages are easier to compare and cheaper to enter. Planners are more flexible and more expensive. Many planners sell both side by side, which makes it easier to start with a package and add custom work as needed.

When should I book a wedding proposal package?

Most planners take new bookings 6 to 12 weeks out. Full-service and destination packages often need 3 to 6 months, especially around weekends and around holidays. The week of Valentine's Day, the week of Christmas, and most weekends in May and June book up first. If your date is fixed and any of those apply, inquire as early as you can.

Do wedding proposal packages include the ring?

No. The engagement ring is always separate. The average US engagement ring spend is about $5,500 according to The Knot (2023), and that purchase belongs with a jeweler you trust, not as a line item on a planning invoice.

Can I customize a wedding proposal package?

Most planners allow add-ons such as extra florals, live music, a videographer, or a chef. Few will subtract from a package to reduce price, because the labor cost is mostly fixed. If you want a stripped-down version of a higher tier, you usually need to move down a tier and add what you actually want as upgrades.

What is the refund policy on a proposal package deposit?

Standard is a 30 to 50 percent non-refundable deposit to hold the date, with the balance due 1 to 2 weeks before the day. Most reputable planners refund the deposit if they cancel and forfeit it if you cancel. Read the cancellation clause line by line before signing. The good firms write it plainly. The bad ones bury it.
